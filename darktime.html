This Is the Dark Time My Love

Martin Carter

    This is the dark time, my love,
    All round the land brown beetles crawl about.
    The shining sun is hidden in the sky
    Red flowers bend their heads in awful sorrow.

    This is the dark time, my love,
    It is the season of oppression, dark metal, and tears.
    It is the festival of guns, the carnival of misery.
    Everywhere the faces of men are strained and anxious.

    Who comes walking in the dark night time?
    Whose boot of steel tramps down the slender grass?
    It is the man of death, my love, the strange invader
    Watching you sleep and aiming at your dream.

Summary

Martin Carter’s This is the Dark Time, My Love is a politically charged poem set during the British colonization of Guyana in 1953, a time of civil unrest and the suspension of constitutional rights. The poem uses rich imagery, personification, and symbolism to convey the oppressive atmosphere caused by invading soldiers. The speaker addresses his “love”—likely a metaphor for his homeland—lamenting how war, violence, and despair have overtaken the country. Nature, normally a source of peace, reflects the sorrow of the people. The poem warns of a sinister force, “the man of death,” who crushes dreams and freedoms underfoot.

Main themes: Colonialism, war, oppression, death, loss of hope.

Tone: Gloomy, mournful, intimate, foreboding.

Line-by-Line Analysis

"This is the dark time, my love,"

The poem opens with a direct and intimate address. The phrase "dark time" signals a period of oppression and sorrow. The term "my love" could refer to a romantic partner but more likely symbolizes the speaker’s love for his country.

"All round the land brown beetles crawl about."

A metaphor for soldiers, likely British troops. Describing them as “brown beetles” dehumanizes them, suggesting an infestation. The alliteration emphasizes their overwhelming presence.

"The shining sun is hidden in the sky
Red flowers bend their heads in awful sorrow."

Nature reflects the national mood. The hidden sun suggests the absence of hope. The red flowers, associated with blood, are personified to express mourning. This reinforces the mood of death and despair.

"It is the season of oppression, dark metal, and tears."

The “season” suggests that this oppression is not brief but prolonged. “Dark metal” likely symbolizes military machinery, such as guns and tanks. The repetition of “dark” builds a somber atmosphere.

"It is the festival of guns, the carnival of misery."

Two oxymorons are used. “Festival” and “carnival” imply celebration, yet they are paired with suffering. This highlights how violence has perverted normal life into something grotesque.

"Everywhere the faces of men are strained and anxious."

This line shows the emotional toll of the conflict. The people, possibly including the speaker himself, are under visible stress. The war has affected all aspects of daily life.

"Who comes walking in the dark night time?
Whose boot of steel tramps down the slender grass?"

These rhetorical questions create suspense and prepare for the introduction of a sinister figure. The “boot of steel” symbolizes military brutality crushing fragile life—represented by the “slender grass.”

"It is the man of death, my love, the strange invader
Watching you sleep and aiming at your dream."

War and death are personified as a threatening intruder. He not only crushes nature, but also aims to destroy the “dream”—representing the hope and aspirations of the people. The imagery is chilling and deeply symbolic.
