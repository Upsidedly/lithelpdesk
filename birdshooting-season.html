Birdshooting Season

Olive Senior

        Birdshooting season the men
        make marriages with their guns
        My father’s house turns macho
        as from far the hunters gather

        All night long contentless women
        stir their brews: hot coffee
        chocolata, cerassie
        wrap pone and tie-leaf
        for tomorrow’s sport. Tonight
        the men drink white rum neat.

        In darkness shouldering
        their packs, their guns, they leave
        We stand quietly on the
        doorstep shivering. Little boys
        longing to grow up birdhunters too
        Little girls whispering
        Fly Birds Fly.

Summary

Olive Senior’s Birdshooting Season explores the annual ritual of bird hunting in a Caribbean village, seen through the eyes of a young girl. As men flock to her father’s house to prepare for the season, the atmosphere becomes one of exaggerated masculinity. Women, meanwhile, are relegated to dutifully preparing food and drink, despite receiving no thanks. The poem contrasts the idolization of men by young boys with the empathy of the girls, who whisper encouragement for the birds to escape. The poem critiques traditional gender roles and highlights how cultural customs reinforce male dominance from childhood.

Main themes: Gender roles, masculinity, culture and tradition, childhood perspectives, nature.

Tone: Reflective, nostalgic, subtly critical.

Line-by-Line Analysis

"Birdshooting season the men / make marriages with their guns"

This opening line sets the scene. The phrase “make marriages with their guns” is a metaphor suggesting the men’s deep attachment to their firearms. It implies that the guns become their primary partners, cared for and revered more than their human relationships. The alliteration of the 'm' sound reinforces the masculine tone and ritualistic nature of this event.

"My father’s house turns macho / as from far the hunters gather"

The speaker’s home is transformed by the presence of many men, creating a charged atmosphere of macho energy — assertive, competitive, and hypermasculine. This reflects how cultural rituals can amplify certain traits and suppress others, especially emotional sensitivity or feminine presence.

"All night long contentless women / stir their brews: hot coffee / chocolata, cerassie"

The women are described as "contentless" — a word that evokes quiet resentment or unspoken dissatisfaction. While the men are celebrated and glorified for their hunting, the women are expected to serve. The inclusion of Caribbean drinks like “chocolata” and “cerassie” adds local cultural flavor and grounds the poem in a specific regional setting.

"wrap pone and tie-leaf / for tomorrow’s sport. Tonight / the men drink white rum neat."

The women prepare traditional foods, like pone and tie-leaf, to sustain the men. Meanwhile, the men drink undiluted white rum — a marker of their perceived toughness. This contrast between the silent labor of the women and the bold behavior of the men reinforces the unequal gender dynamics.

"In darkness shouldering / their packs, their guns, they leave"

The men leave in the early morning, cloaked in darkness. The line evokes a militaristic image — as if these men are going off to war. The guns are again emphasized, showing how central they are to the ritual and the identity of the men.

"We stand quietly on the / doorstep shivering."

The children — likely including the female speaker — are left behind in the cold. Their presence on the doorstep signifies both emotional and physical distance from the world of the men. The shivering suggests vulnerability and perhaps fear or discomfort.

"Little boys / longing to grow up birdhunters too"

The young boys idolize the hunters, aspiring to replicate the same displays of masculinity. This highlights how gender roles are passed down and reinforced from childhood — a subtle form of social conditioning.

"Little girls whispering: / Fly Birds Fly."

In contrast, the girls hope the birds will escape. This symbolic contrast between boys and girls reveals differing emotional responses: the boys embrace the masculine ideal, while the girls resist it in quiet solidarity with the hunted. The girls may also identify with the birds themselves — vulnerable beings who want to avoid being caught or controlled.
