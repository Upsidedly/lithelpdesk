Comparative Analysis: Dulce et Decorum Est vs This Is the Dark Time, My Love

Introduction

Wilfred Owen’s Dulce et Decorum Est and Martin Carter’s This Is the Dark Time, My Love are powerful anti-war poems that expose the brutal consequences of conflict on individuals and society. Though written in different contexts—World War I and British colonial Guyana respectively—both poems highlight themes of war, suffering, death, and the loss of innocence and hope. Using vivid imagery and mournful tones, the poets condemn the destructive nature of militarism and its impact on human life.

1. War and Its Horrors

Both poets portray war not as a noble pursuit, but as a source of intense suffering and dehumanization.

  • In Dulce et Decorum Est, Owen describes the soldiers as “bent double, like old beggars,” stripping them of any glory. War is presented as traumatizing and grotesque, with “froth-corrupted lungs” and “gargling blood” painting a horrifying picture.
  • Similarly, Carter’s poem speaks of a “festival of guns” and a “carnival of misery,” using oxymoron to show how violence has warped everyday life. His metaphor “brown beetles crawl about” reduces soldiers to pests, suggesting an invasion and infestation.

2. Suffering and Oppression

Suffering is central in both poems, not just physical but emotional and psychological.

  • Owen emphasizes emotional trauma, shown in “In all my dreams, before my helpless sight.” The speaker cannot escape the haunting image of his dying comrade.
  • Carter presents collective suffering—a national anguish. “Everywhere the faces of men are strained and anxious” shows how everyone is affected by the military occupation.

3. Death and Loss of Hope

Death looms heavily in both works, often paired with lost hope or dreams.

  • In Owen’s poem, death is graphic and traumatic: “choking, drowning,” with the bitter line “the old Lie” dismissing the idea of glory in death.
  • In Carter’s, death is more symbolic and lurking: “the man of death... watching you sleep and aiming at your dream,” illustrating the death of hope and liberty rather than just the body.

4. Imagery and Tone

The imagery in both poems is dark, disturbing, and emotionally charged.

  • Owen uses graphic similes and metaphors: “like a man in fire or lime,” “like old beggars,” and “as under a green sea,” to immerse the reader in the battlefield’s horror.
  • Carter uses symbolic and surreal imagery: “red flowers bend their heads in awful sorrow” and “boot of steel tramps down the slender grass,” to reflect emotional and national trauma.

Their tones are also aligned:

  • Owen’s tone: Bitter and accusatory.
  • Carter’s tone: Intimate and mournful, but foreboding.

5. Criticism of Power and Propaganda

Both poems directly or indirectly criticize the structures that enable violence.

  • Owen’s “my friend, you would not tell with such high zest…” is a direct attack on those who glorify war, especially aimed at the propaganda that misleads young soldiers.
  • Carter’s “strange invader” and “man of death” reference colonial forces, showing how foreign powers suppress and terrorize the people.

Conclusion

Despite differing in historical context and poetic style, Dulce et Decorum Est and This Is the Dark Time, My Love share a common purpose: to expose the cruelty of war and the suffering it inflicts. Through their vivid imagery, mournful tones, and piercing criticisms of authority, Owen and Carter reject the romanticism of war and highlight its devastating consequences—making both poems enduring and powerful anti-war statements in the CSEC poetry collection.
