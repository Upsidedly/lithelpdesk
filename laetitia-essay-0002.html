Life of Laetitia Essay #0002

Parental figures shape the direction of a child’s life.
Write an essay in which you compare the parenting styles of TWO adults from the novel. In your essay, assess how their approach affects ONE child and discuss how the author uses contrast to make this point.

A child’s future can either be stunted or uplifted by the kind of parenting they receive. In Merle Hodge’s novel For the Life of Laetitia, two adult figures — Cephas and Lacey — offer drastically different models of parenting, both of which significantly affect the development of Michael, Cephas’ son. While Cephas is a cold, authoritarian father who fails to nurture his children emotionally or socially, Lacey presents a warm, firm, and consistent approach to parenting that encourages discipline and growth. Through the contrast between these two figures, Hodge emphasizes that the success and character of a child are not only shaped by blood relations, but by the presence of positive, supportive guidance.

Cephas’ parenting style is harsh, neglectful, and rooted in patriarchal control. He is often described as self-important and arrogant, with a clear disdain for emotional connection and a limited view of what children, especially boys, should be. His parenting is more about maintaining dominance and order than about cultivating love or understanding. As a result, Michael, who is raised under his influence, becomes ill-mannered, unhygienic, and inconsiderate. He shows no respect for his personal space or for others, especially women, reflecting Cephas’ own dismissive behavior. This begins to establish in Michael a warped sense of masculinity and an unhealthy pattern of behaviour that could lead him to become just as oppressive as his father.

On the other hand, Lacey’s parenting is marked by emotional intelligence, patience, and quiet strength. Although not Michael’s biological parent, Lacey assumes the role of a caregiver when she moves in with Cephas. Unlike him, she takes the time to gently correct Michael’s behavior, teaching him to be tidy, respectful, and responsible. She provides discipline without cruelty and shows that love does not have to be loud or aggressive to be effective. Importantly, Lacey does not mirror Cephas’ authoritarianism — instead, she sets boundaries while maintaining empathy. As a result, Michael begins to change: he becomes more helpful, aware of his behavior, and open to growth. This demonstrates the impact a nurturing environment can have, even when a child has already been shaped by a negative one.

Hodge uses contrast between Cephas and Lacey to emphasize the transformative power of parenting. Cephas represents the old-world, emotionally distant father figure whose presence harms more than helps. Lacey, meanwhile, embodies the positive maternal presence that encourages growth and healing. The juxtaposition of their approaches creates a clear narrative message: that children become the product of what they are exposed to, and while neglect breeds resentment and disorder, love and discipline can foster maturity and self-respect.

In conclusion, Cephas’ neglectful, overbearing parenting style sets Michael on a path of emotional underdevelopment and poor social behavior. Lacey’s gentle but firm intervention, however, redirects him towards becoming a more respectful and responsible person. Through the contrast between these two parental figures, Hodge underscores that a child’s character is not predestined but is instead shaped by the adults who raise them — for better or for worse.
